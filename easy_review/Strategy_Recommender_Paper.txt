**Context-Aware Strategy Recommendation in Swiss Chess Tournaments Using Machine Learning**
## Abstract

## 1. Introduction
The Elo rating system (Elo, 1978) provides a calibrated probability for the outcome of a single chess game given two ratings, and remains the foundation of competitive chess. In Swiss-system tournaments (Hua, 2017), however, players accumulate scores over several rounds and are paired adaptively against opponents on similar scores. A player's final standing therefore depends not only on per-game probabilities but on the unfolding tournament context — current standing, average opponent strength so far, rounds remaining, color balance, and the structural pairing dynamics of the field.
Recent machine-learning work on chess has focused mainly on per-game outcome classification from rating and metadata features (Samara et al., 2025). Tournament-level decision-making — when to take risks, when to consolidate, when to simplify toward a draw — is well understood by coaches but has received little quantitative treatment.
We ask whether the round-by-round strategic stance a player adopts (aggressive, solid, or passive) carries learnable signal for tournament-level outcomes once rating information is controlled for. We frame the problem as regression on a player's final performance rating (Rp) given (situation, candidate action), train an XGBoost model on data from 150 FIDE-rated Swiss tournaments scraped from chess-results.com (147 surviving the labeling step), and expose it as a per-round strategy recommender. The system is intended as a decision aid, consistent with evidence that human–AI teams perform best when models complement rather than substitute for expertise (Inkpen et al., 2023).
## 2. Methods
Data. We scraped 150 FIDE-rated Swiss tournaments from chess-results.com, yielding 9,098 players and 35,728 games (validation_report.txt). Tournaments span December 2024 to December 2025, with 72 events in 2024 and 78 in 2025; the largest federations represented are India, Germany, Argentina, Turkey, Spain, and the United Kingdom. Field sizes range from 30 to 150 players (mean 68.4) and rounds per tournament from 6 to 11. Candidate events are identified by a separate discovery script (scrape_discovery.py); run_pipeline.sh then downloads pairings and crosstables, parses them to SQLite, validates, engineers features, assigns action labels, and trains.

## 3. Results
Predictive accuracy. On the 29 held-out test tournaments (12,648 rows), the bracket-mean baseline reached MAE = 168.1 Elo points and the Elo-formula baseline reached MAE = 144.7 (training_report.txt). XGBoost reached MAE = 94.3 — a 73.8-point improvement over Baseline 0 and a 50.4-point improvement over Baseline 1, equivalent to a 35% reduction against the stronger baseline (Figure 1, mae_comparison.png). The best cross-validated MAE during hyperparameter search was 88.5; the small CV-to-test gap (5.8 points) suggests limited overfitting given 118 training tournaments, in contrast to earlier 10-tournament runs that produced markedly larger gaps (final_report.ipynb). The selected configuration was 136 trees of max depth 6, learning_rate 0.0458, subsample 0.7115, colsample_bytree 0.8119, min_child_weight 5, gamma 0.1209, reg_alpha 0.8826, reg_lambda 0.8774.

SHAP analysis (Figure 3, shap_summary.png) confirms direction: after controlling for player and opponent strength, action_passive contributes a consistently downward push to predicted Rp when active, action_aggressive contributes an upward push, and score_delta interacts strongly with current_score — players running ahead of Elo expectation receive a larger positive Rp adjustment.

## 4. Discussion
The model adds genuine predictive value beyond rating-only baselines. A 50.4-point MAE reduction against the FIDE Elo baseline indicates that tournament context — not just rating identity — carries learnable signal for end-of-event Rp.

Several limitations should be made explicit. (1) Action labels are heuristic, derived from result-vs-Elo-expectation patterns rather than move-level chess analysis; a fortunate win in a drawn position can be labeled aggressive when no strategic risk was actually taken. (2) The dataset is restricted to Swiss-system events; round-robin and match play have different scoring incentives and pairing dynamics and would require separate validation. (3) chess-results.com publishes only results and ratings — no game scores — so move-quality signals (engine evaluation, blunder rate, opening choice, time pressure) are absent and likely informative. (4) Performance rating is one summary statistic; some tournament objectives (title norms, prize-stratified standings) diverge from maximizing round-by-round Rp. (5) Sample timing spans December 2024 to December 2025 (72 and 78 events respectively) and is therefore vulnerable to temporal drift.
