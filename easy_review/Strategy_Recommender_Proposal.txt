Context-Aware Strategy Recommendation in Swiss Chess Tournaments Using Machine Learning

Capstone Proposal

1. Introduction

The Elo rating system [1] provides a calibrated probability for the outcome of a single chess game given two ratings, and remains the foundation of competitive chess. In Swiss-system tournaments [2], however, players accumulate scores over several rounds and are paired adaptively against opponents on similar scores. A player's final standing therefore depends not only on per-game probabilities but on the unfolding tournament context — current standing, average opponent strength so far, rounds remaining, color balance, and the structural pairing dynamics of the field.

Strategic stance is also part of this picture. Experienced players adapt their approach round-by-round: pressing for a win when the tournament situation rewards risk, consolidating with a solid line against a stronger opponent, or accepting a quiet draw when standing already permits it. These choices are routinely made by competitors and coaches, but rarely modeled quantitatively.

This capstone proposes a context-aware strategy recommender for Swiss-system chess tournaments. The system maps a player's current situation and a candidate action — aggressive, solid, or passive — to a predicted end-of-tournament performance rating (Rp), and surfaces the action that maximizes that prediction together with the features driving it. The intent is a decision aid for the player, not an autonomous decision-maker.

2. Literature Review

Existing machine-learning work on chess has focused largely on per-game outcome classification from rating and metadata features [3]. These approaches treat each game as an independent prediction problem and do not condition on tournament-level state such as current standing, color balance, or remaining rounds.

The Elo system [1] itself provides a strong rating-based baseline, but is built on the single-game expectation assumption and does not exploit accumulating within-tournament information. The Swiss pairing mechanism [2] further introduces adaptive opponent selection that breaks game-level independence, but Elo does not model this either.

XGBoost [4] is the established gradient-boosted tree method for tabular regression with mixed feature types — static identity, tournament context, and prior-round state — and is a natural fit for the present setting. Tree boosting is also well-suited to the SHAP attribution framework used below, which is required to expose the recommender's reasoning rather than its output alone.

Work on human–AI joint decision making [5] indicates that interpretable output — recommendations paired with the reasons behind them — outperforms opaque predictions when the end user retains decision authority. This shapes how the recommender is exposed: as a per-round suggestion with SHAP-derived drivers, scored against the player's own judgment.

3. Problem Statement

The research question is whether the round-by-round strategic stance a player adopts — aggressive, solid, or passive — carries learnable signal for tournament-level outcomes once rating information is controlled for.

The framing has two parts. First, prediction: does context beyond rating reduce error in Rp prediction. Second, recommendation: can the trained model be inverted into a per-round action recommender by re-scoring the situation under each candidate action and returning the one with the highest predicted Rp.

4. Methods

Data. 150 FIDE-rated Swiss tournaments are scraped from chess-results.com, yielding 9,098 players and 35,728 games. Tournaments cluster in December 2025; the largest federations represented are India, Germany, Argentina, Turkey, Spain, and the United Kingdom. Field sizes range from 30 to 150 players (mean 68.4) and rounds per tournament from 6 to 11. A single pipeline was built to discover candidate events, download pairings and crosstables, parse them to SQLite, validate, engineer features, assign action labels, and train.
